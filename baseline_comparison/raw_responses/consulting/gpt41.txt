## 1. **General Assessment**

**Strengths:**
- The agreement is comprehensive, covering all major aspects of a technology consulting relationship.
- It defines key terms and incorporates flexibility via specific Statements of Work (SOWs).
- It delineates IP rights, confidentiality, termination, liability, and dispute resolution.
- It includes a mutual non-solicitation clause and appropriate insurance requirements.

**Potential Weaknesses / Considerations:**
- Some wording could be clarified or expanded, depending on each party’s relative bargaining power and risk tolerance.
- Jurisdiction is limited to Texas courts, which may not be convenient for the Nevada-based Client.
- Payment terms/interest and scope of indemnification could merit close attention.

## 2. **Section-by-Section Review & Recommendations**

### **Article 1 - Definitions**
- Very clear and helpful. Ensure SOWs also clearly define any roles, milestones, and specific details of Deliverables/Services.

### **Article 2 - Services**
- **Change Orders (2.3):** Sensible to require written changes. Both Parties should set timelines for responding to change order requests to avoid delay.
- **Client Cooperation (2.4):** May wish to clarify consequences if Client fails a material obligation to cooperate (e.g., excusing Consultant from delays/performance issues).

### **Article 3 - Fees and Expenses**
- **Expenses (3.2):** Sensible $500 threshold on pre-approval. However, periodic/aggregate expense limits could be considered for budgeting.
- **Invoicing and Payment (3.3/3.4):** Standard. Interest on late payments is reasonable, but check state usury laws.

### **Article 4 - Independent Contractor**
- **Taxes and Benefits (4.2):** Consultant covers taxes, not eligible for Client employee benefits.
- **Control (4.3):** Makes clear Consultant retains control over the means/methods of work.

### **Article 5 - Intellectual Property**
- **Ownership (5.1):** Work-for-hire, but only for Deliverables “specifically for Client.” Anything not in Deliverables (e.g., drafts, approaches, tools) remains Consultant’s.
    - *Recommendation:* For the Client, ensure the SOW and Deliverables' scope is broad to cover everything you will need exclusive access to. For Consultant, maintain adequate documentation of pre-existing IP/background IP.
- **Background IP (5.2):** Consultant keeps all rights, but grants Client a **broad license** to incorporated Background IP, limited to use “as part of the Deliverables.”
    - *Recommendation (Client):* Consider whether you’ll need rights to modify/support/transfer Deliverables in the future.
- **Third-Party Materials (5.3):** Consultant must notify Client and ensure licenses. Good protection for Client.

### **Article 6 - Confidentiality**
- Standard and adequate. Preserves mutual obligations.
- **Return/Destruction (6.4):** Allows exception for retained information for legal/regulatory reasons.

### **Article 7 - Term and Termination**
- **Termination for Convenience (7.1):** Either side can terminate on 30 days’ notice.
    - *Note:* If the Client needs continuity for critical projects, consider limiting unilateral convenience termination during major phases.
- **Effect of Termination (7.4):** Client pays for all work/expenses until termination, Consultant turns over paid-for Deliverables.
    - *Question:* What happens with partially completed Deliverables? Should ensure Client has IP rights to in-progress work—even if incomplete.

### **Article 8 - Representations and Warranties**
- **Consultant Warranties:** Standard, but limited. No “time period” specified for warranty period (consider requesting post-acceptance window for defect correction).
- **Disclaimer:** Standard, but clients often seek assurances on fitness for purpose.

### **Article 9 - Limitation of Liability**
- **Exclusion:** No indirect, special, or consequential damages.
- **Cap:** Liability is capped at the fees paid or payable under the applicable SOW (*except* for confidentiality and indemnity).
    - *Recommendation (Client):* Consider whether this provides sufficient protection for anticipated risk; in certain high-value contracts, higher caps may be negotiated.
    - *Consultant should ensure:* No “uncapped” exposure except for confidentiality and indemnification (which is standard).

### **Article 10 - Indemnification**
- Standard, mutual, and fair. Consultant covers IP infringement and their own negligence/wilful misconduct; Client covers unauthorized use and their own negligence/wilful misconduct.

### **Article 11 - Insurance**
- Reasonable coverage amounts. Client should request to be named as additional insured where applicable.

### **Article 12 - Non-Solicitation**
- Standard. 12-month period is typical.
- Allows general recruiting activities, which is fair.

### **Article 13 - General Provisions**
- Standard, thoughtful, and comprehensive.
    - **Governing Law/Jurisdiction:** Texas law / Travis County. Confirm both parties’ willingness to litigate there.
    - **Assignment:** Reasonable; allows assignment in connection with M&A type activities.
    - **Entire Agreement / Waiver / Severability / Counterparts / Survival:** All as industry standard.

## 3. **Other Recommendations**

- **Data Security:** If sensitive data will be accessed, consider adding express data security or data protection obligations.
- **Acceptance Criteria:** Recommend specifying objective acceptance criteria/procedures for Deliverables in the SOW.
- **Subcontracting:** Are there restrictions or notification requirements for Consultant’s use of subcontractors?
- **Force Majeure:** Consider adding a clause excusing non-performance for events such as natural disasters, war, etc.
