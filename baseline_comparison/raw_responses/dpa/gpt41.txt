## **Strengths**

**1. Legal Basis & Structure**
- Reflects GDPR/UK GDPR requirements for controller-processor relationships (Articles 28, 32, etc.).
- Starts with clear definitions, identifies both parties, references the principal agreement (good practice).
- Governing law and conflict clause clearly stated (Art. 11.1, 11.2).

**2. Sub-Processors**
- Provides for general authorisation and a right to object (Art. 4.1), which is compliant, and clarifies processor liability (Art. 4.3).
- Requires data protection obligations to “equivalent” standards (Art. 4.2), as required by GDPR.

**3. Security Standards**
- ISO 27001 or “equivalent” required (Art. 5.2); demonstrates a baseline and a quantifiable expectation.
- Encrypted, access controls, regular testing (Art. 5.1) correctly referenced.

**4. International Transfers**
- Transfer only with legal safeguards in place (Art. 6.1, 6.2).

**5. Audit Rights and Data Breach Procedures**
- Audit clause allows for oversight while limiting burden (Art. 7).
- 72-hour breach notification aligns with GDPR Article 33.

**6. Data Subject Rights & Assistance**
- Assistance in responding to requests and fulfilling controller obligations (Art. 3.3).

**7. End-of-Processing Obligations**
- Requires processor to delete or return all data (Art. 3.4/10.2) unless legal requirements dictate otherwise.

## **Areas for Consideration/Improvement**

### **1. [Controller’s Instructions – Article 2.3]**
- **Best practice:** Specify a mechanism for communicating/recording instructions and a requirement to alert Controller if instructions breach laws.
    - *Suggestion:* Add: “If the Processor considers an instruction to be in breach of Data Protection Laws, it shall immediately inform the Controller.”

### **2. [Assistance Obligations – Article 3.3]**
- Consider referencing support with DPIAs (Data Protection Impact Assessments) and consultation with authorities, as required under GDPR Art. 28(3)(f).

### **3. [Objection Process for Sub-processors – Article 4.1]**
- What happens if the Controller objects? Is there a process to resolve?
    - *Suggestion*: “If the Controller reasonably objects to a Sub-processor, the parties shall seek to find a commercially reasonable solution. If no solution is found, Controller may terminate the relevant Services.”

### **4. [Security Clause – Article 5]**
- Exhibit B is referenced but left blank. The DPA is not complete without a concrete description of technical and organizational measures (GDPR Art 32). This should be appended as soon as possible.
- Consider requiring notification if security certifications lapse.

### **5. [Personal Data Breach Notification – Article 8]**
- Consider specifying communication methods (e.g., email, phone), and notification even in the event of a likely non-reportable breach (to ensure the controller can decide).
- Consider adding: “Processor shall provide regular updates as further information becomes available.”

### **6. [Liability and Indemnity – Article 9]**
- The indemnity is broadly drafted. Some processors may want to limit this to breaches arising from their fault/negligence and may seek to exclude indirect/consequential losses or cap liability.
- Depending on bargaining positions, parties may want to define liability caps. Controllers may prefer it uncapped (especially for regulatory fines); processors may wish to discuss.

### **7. [Miscellaneous / General]**
- **Exhibits**: A, B, and C must be completed for the contract to work in practice (the DPA references these for essential information).
- **Signatories/Authority**: Ensure the signatory is authorised for each party.

## **Additional Recommendations**

- **Record of Processing**: While not required, Processor might offer to maintain a record and provide this upon request (to support Art. 30).
- **Jurisdiction**: For multinational Controllers, check that English law is appropriate for all parties.
- **Assignment**: Consider if assignment/transfer of DPA is permitted.
- **Order of Precedence**: Clause 11.2 is good but could be more explicit: “With respect to the subject matter hereof, this DPA prevails over any conflicting provisions in the Principal Agreement.”
